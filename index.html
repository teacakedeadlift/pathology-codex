GASTROINTESTINAL STROMAL TUMOUR (GIST)
LGI / UGI Mesenchymal Spindle cell Variable
Derived from interstitial cells of Cajal (pacemaker cells of GI tract).
Clinical Context
  • Derived from interstitial cells of Cajal (pacemaker cells of GI tract).
  • Most common GI mesenchymal tumour in adults.
  • Stomach 60%, small bowel 30%, colorectum/oesophagus <10%.
  • Intramural, submucosal, or subserosal location.
  • Most often spindled (70%); epithelioid (20%) or mixed.
  • Trigger: incidental mass; GI bleed; obstruction.
Key Microscopic Features
  • Spindle cells in short fascicles/storiform pattern; perinuclear vacuolation (cytoplasmic vacuoles flanking nucleus) — characteristic.
  • Skeinoid fibres (extracellular collagen globules) — especially small intestinal GISTs; nuclear palisading may mimic schwannoma.
  • Epithelioid variant: round/polygonal cells, clear or eosinophilic cytoplasm; multinodular growth in SDH-deficient; multinucleate giant cells.

Comments — Molecular / Risk / Management
  • Prognostication: size + mitotic count (per 5mm²/20HPF, highest density area) + site. Use NIH/Miettinen criteria.
  • KIT exon 11 del/ins (50%) – most common; best imatinib response. Exon 9 – double-dose imatinib. Exon 17/18 – imatinib resistance.
  • PDGFRA exon 18 D842V (10%) – good prognosis; imatinib-resistant → avapritinib.
  • Wild-type (10%): SDH-deficient, NF1-associated, BRAF V600E (~10% of wt), NTRK-fused — all imatinib-insensitive.
  • SDH-deficient: young adults/children; gastric; multinodular/multifocal; LVI common but indolent; lymph node mets (unique). SDHB IHC loss = surrogate.
  • Syndromic: NF1 (multiple small bowel GISTs); Carney triad (GIST + pulmonary chondroma + paraganglioma); Carney-Stratakis (GIST + paraganglioma, SDH germline, AD).
  • NACT: imatinib pre-op to downsize. ACT: all high-risk (adjuvant imatinib 3 years).
  • Post-imatinib: CD117 may be lost; DOG1 retained. No lymph node mets except SDH-deficient.
  • Surgical margins most important prognostic factor in non-metastatic disease. Mutational analysis mandatory for all high-risk/metastatic.

GI SCHWANNOMA
LGI / UGI Mesenchymal Spindle cell Benign
Benign peripheral nerve sheath tumour of Schwannian differentiation.
Clinical Context
  • Benign peripheral nerve sheath tumour of Schwannian differentiation.
  • Stomach 60-70%, then colorectum; oesophagus rare.
  • Adults; no sex predilection.
  • Often incidental — radiologists frequently call these GISTs.
  • No NF2 association (unlike conventional schwannoma).
Key Microscopic Features
  • Spindle cells in loose fascicles; uniform elongated nuclei; NO Antoni A/B, NO Verocay bodies, NO thick-walled hyalinised vessels.
  • Characteristic peritumoural lymphoid cuff — virtually diagnostic.
  • Variants: microcystic/reticular (GFAP+); signet-ring cell variant (intracytoplasmic mucin, EMA−).

  • H. pylori gastritis association reported (controversial).

LEIOMYOMA (GI)
LGI / UGI Mesenchymal Spindle cell Benign
Most common mesenchymal tumour of oesophagus (muscularis propria) and colorectum (muscularis mucosae).
Clinical Context
  • Most common mesenchymal tumour of oesophagus (muscularis propria) and colorectum (muscularis mucosae).
  • Stomach uncommon (unlike GIST). Anorectal: muscularis mucosae, women.
  • Adults; oesophageal favours men.
  • Usually incidental; may present as intramural mass or polyp.
  • Syndromic: oesophago-gastric leiomyomatosis in Alport syndrome (COL4A5/COL4A6 deletion at Xq22).
Key Microscopic Features
  • Fascicles of bland spindle cells with abundant eosinophilic fibrillar cytoplasm (pink at low power); cigar-shaped blunt-ended nuclei.
  • Low cellularity, minimal nuclear atypia, no/rare mitoses (<1/50HPF), no necrosis.
  • Secondary hyperplasia of interstitial cells of Cajal at periphery (focal CD117+ staining — do not misdiagnose as GIST).

LEIOMYOSARCOMA (GI)
LGI / UGI Mesenchymal Spindle cell Malignant
Rare in GI tract; most retroperitoneal/somatic soft tissue.
Clinical Context
  • Rare in GI tract; most retroperitoneal/somatic soft tissue.
  • Stomach and small bowel most common GI sites; can arise from muscularis propria or vascular wall.
  • Adults (median 6th-7th decade).
  • IMPORTANT: exclude GIST first — most spindle cell GI sarcomas are GIST.
Key Microscopic Features
  • Intersecting fascicles of spindle cells at right angles; moderate-to-severe nuclear atypia; hyperchromatic cigar-shaped nuclei.
  • Mitoses ≥5/50HPF (often very high); atypical mitoses; coagulative necrosis common.
  • Eosinophilic fibrillar cytoplasm; epithelioid morphology possible (worse prognosis).

  • Vascular leiomyosarcoma (IVC, pulmonary veins): distinct site-specific entity.

INFLAMMATORY FIBROID POLYP (IFP)
LGI / UGI Mesenchymal Myxoid Benign
Benign intramural/submucosal lesion; not a true inflammatory process.
Clinical Context
  • Benign intramural/submucosal lesion; not a true inflammatory process.
  • Stomach (antrum) most common; also terminal ileum (can cause intussusception); colorectum rare.
  • Adults; mean age ~50.
  • Ileum: intussusception (lead point) — classic trigger phrase.
  • Associated with H. pylori (gastric).
Key Microscopic Features
  • Loose oedematous myxoid stroma with bland spindle/stellate cells; 'onion-skin' perivascular whorling — characteristic.
  • Prominent eosinophilic infiltrate throughout stroma; mast cells.
  • Thin-walled vessels; no atypia; no/rare mitoses.

DESMOID TUMOUR / INTRA-ABDOMINAL FIBROMATOSIS
LGI Mesenchymal Fibrous/collagenous Intermediate
Locally aggressive myofibroblastic neoplasm; no metastatic potential.
Clinical Context
  • Locally aggressive myofibroblastic neoplasm; no metastatic potential.
  • Mesenteric: 50% associated with FAP/APC (Gardner); sporadic ~50% (CTNNB1).
  • FAP-associated: younger; follows colectomy; mesentery; multiple.
  • Sporadic: young women; abdominal wall; prior trauma/surgery/pregnancy.
  • Trigger: FAP patient with abdominal mass post-colectomy; Gardner syndrome.
Key Microscopic Features
  • Long sweeping fascicles of bland fibroblasts/myofibroblasts with abundant collagen; low-to-moderate cellularity.
  • Uniform elongated nuclei; small nucleoli; vesicular chromatin; no significant atypia; no/rare mitoses; no necrosis.
  • Infiltrative margins — tongues into surrounding fat/muscle; may encase mesenteric vessels.

INFLAMMATORY MYOFIBROBLASTIC TUMOUR (IMT)
LGI / UGI Mesenchymal Myxoid Intermediate
Intermediate malignant potential: ~25% local recurrence; rare distant mets (~5%).
Clinical Context
  • Intermediate malignant potential: ~25% local recurrence; rare distant mets (~5%).
  • Children and young adults (median ~10 years); most common mesenchymal tumour of lung in children.
  • Mesentery, omentum, retroperitoneum, lung; GI primary uncommon.
  • Systemic symptoms: fever, weight loss, anaemia, thrombocytosis (paraneoplastic IL-6).
  • Trigger: young patient, soft tissue mass, systemic inflammatory symptoms.
Key Microscopic Features
  • Three patterns: (1) myxoid/vascular — loose stroma, abundant plasma cells + lymphocytes; (2) compact spindle cell — dense fascicles; (3) hypocellular fibrous — collagen-rich.
  • Spindle to epithelioid myofibroblasts; vesicular nuclei; prominent nucleoli; mild-to-moderate atypia.
  • Mixed inflammatory infiltrate (plasma cells, lymphocytes, eosinophils) — always present.

GLOMUS TUMOUR (GI)
UGI Mesenchymal Perivascular Benign
Perivascular smooth muscle neoplasm differentiating toward glomus cells.
Clinical Context
  • Perivascular smooth muscle neoplasm differentiating toward glomus cells.
  • Stomach is the most common GI site (>90%); antrum/body.
  • Adults; wide age range; no sex predilection.
  • Usually submucosal; often incidental endoscopic finding.
  • Trigger: gastric submucosal mass, GIST excluded on IHC.
Key Microscopic Features
  • Sheets/nests of monotonous round cells; central round nuclei, inconspicuous nucleoli; pale-to-clear cytoplasm with sharp cell borders.
  • Prominent perivascular growth — tumour cells surround capillary-sized thin-walled vessels.
  • Hyalinised/myxoid stroma; no nuclear atypia; mitoses rare; no necrosis.

SYNOVIAL SARCOMA (GI)
LGI / UGI Mesenchymal Biphasic Malignant
Rare in GI tract; defined by SS18-SSX translocation regardless of site.
Clinical Context
  • Rare in GI tract; defined by SS18-SSX translocation regardless of site.
  • Oesophagus and stomach most reported GI sites.
  • Young adults (peak 15-40 years); male predominance.

  • Mast cells often prominent; focal calcification; necrosis in high-grade.

WELL-DIFFERENTIATED NET G1/G2 (GI)
LGI / UGI Neuroendocrine Neuroendocrine Variable
WHO 2022: G1 Ki67 <3%, mitoses <2/2mm²; G2 Ki67 3-20%, mitoses 2-20/2mm².
Clinical Context
  • WHO 2022: G1 Ki67 <3%, mitoses <2/2mm²; G2 Ki67 3-20%, mitoses 2-20/2mm².
  • Site-specific biology: ileal (midgut) — serotonin-producing; carcinoid syndrome only with liver mets. Rectal — L-cell (glucagon/PYY); usually small, low risk. Gastric Type 1 — ECL cell hyperplasia, chronic atrophic gastritis/autoimmune; Type 2 — ZES/MEN1; Type 3 — sporadic, aggressive. Appendiceal — most common appendiceal neoplasm; tip; overwhelmingly G1.
  • Appendiceal NET <2cm: appendicectomy curative. >2cm: right hemicolectomy.
  • Carcinoid syndrome: flushing, diarrhoea, bronchospasm — only with hepatic/systemic mets (serotonin bypasses portal metabolism).
  • Trigger: incidental polyp; Octreoscan/68Ga-DOTATATE avid lesion; elevated CgA/5-HIAA.

Comments — Molecular / Risk / Management
  • Arises from parafollicular C cells; accounts for ~5% of thyroid malignancies.
